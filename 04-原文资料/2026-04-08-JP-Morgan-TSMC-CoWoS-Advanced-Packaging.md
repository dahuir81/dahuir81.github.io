slightly and now expect TSMC to have 115K/155K/175K wfpm CoWoS

capacity to slowly ramp up to ~25K/35K wfpm by end-27/end-28. CoPoS (or

capacity expectations materially and now expect SoIC capacity to reach 
35K/65K wfpm by end-27/28, to reflect growing demand from 2nm class AI-

believe multiple AI accelerators (TPU v9, Trainium 4, future generations of

TSMC’s COUPE platform will also be an incremental driver for SoIC demand, 
given that TSMC will be using SoIC for EIC-PIC integration.
•Trim NVDA 2026 estimates to reflect higher Blackwell and lower Rubin, 
no change to 2027 estimates: We trim our 2026 CoWoS estimates for NVDA 
by 4% (now 675K wafers per year) to reflect higher Blackwell shipments 
(5.8M units, up from 4.3M previously) and a slower ramp for Rubin (2.2M 
units, down from 2.8M previously), due primarily to HBM4 availability issues. 
Overall, NVDA GPU shipments are still likely to grow faster than prior 
estimates, in our view, reaching 8.3M units, up 34% YoY . We raise our CoWoS 
estimates for 2027 by 3% and now model all Rubin Ultra GPUs on a 2-die per 
interposer basis, given recent changes. We expect 4-die per package to be the 
mainstream solution, and the choice of 2-die or 4-die per package is unlikely 
to be material to the CoWoS allocation (which is based on a Chip-on-Wafer 
capacity basis). Moving to a 2-die-per-interposer solution may be more 
efficient for GPU chip output, given better yields (~10 dies per CoWoS wafer, 
similar to Rubin). Overall, we believe 2027 CoWoS forecasts are likely 
conservative and could move up in the next few quarters if NVDA is able to 
secure sufficient quantities of other bottleneck components (N3P wafers, 
HBM4), especially given that build plans for Vera CPU appear quite 
conservative, given extensive usage in Vera Rubin GPU racks and NVDA’s 
intent to sell standalone Vera CPU racks. We believe this could be largely 
leveraging OSAT CoWoS-R or equivalent capacity (mainly ASE and Amkor).

•Strong upward revisions to Broadcom CoWoS estimates, primarily for TPU v7/v8:  
We meaningfully revise up our 2026/27 CoWoS forecasts for Broadcom to 250K/400K, 
stemming largely from upside to TPU units. We now expect TPU shipments to reach 
4.3M/6.9M units in 2026/27, with meaningful upside for Broadcom's Ironwood (TPUv7)

forecasts for MediaTek (18K/60K CoWoS wafers in 2026E/27E, implying 0.4M/1.5M 
TPU v8x (Zebrafish) units). MediaTek is still facing small changes to its Zebrafish design 
and could see some changes to this shipment schedule if the issues are not completely 
settled by 1H26. For TPUv9, our supply chain checks indicate that Google’s design teams 
appear to be working on two competing designs with largely similar functional designs 
- Pumafish from Broadcom, based on 3D SoIC and TSMC CoWoS-L, and Humufish from 
MediaTek, using TSMC 3D SoIC, but with 2.5D packaging moving to Intel EMIB-T. At 
this point, we are unclear about which project gets higher volumes, but MediaTek’s 
project looks more risky to us, given the adoption of multiple leading-edge technologies, 
including: (1) high-speed Serdes, with MediaTek aiming for a 300G class Serdes spec 
(336GHz likely, in our view) vs. Broadcom 400G Serdes; (2) Intel EMIB packaging 
execution due to no track record with AI accelerators with multiple embedded silicon 
bridges; and (3) 3D SoIC packaging design, with tape-outs of multiple compute and I/O 
dies  within a short design window.
•Trainium3 lifetime volumes stronger, slightly pushed out into 2027:  We trim our 
CoWoS forecasts for Trn3 (at AWS/Alchip) by 13%, but raise our 2027 estimates by 19%, 
to factor in higher Trn3 demand, but also a slight pushout of volume ramp in 2Q26. We 
now expect lifetime volumes to reach 4.4M units, slightly higher than prior estimates, due 
to strong compute demand from Anthropic and internal AWS workloads. For Trainium 
3, we see almost all CoWoS wafers being processed by either AWS or Alchip directly, 
with no third fabless player in the mix. Given tight demand and TSMC’s reluctance to 
expand CoWoS-R capacity, we see the possibility of some Trn3 volumes being 
outsourced to ASE in 2027 if the company can successfully ramp production of CoWoS 
process with high RDL layer count (>6-8 metal layers).
•Lowering AMD CoWoS numbers slightly for 2026E, no change for 2027E: As 
suggested in our prior update, we have seen some delays at TSMC for MI450 (including 
2nm top-die re-tape-out and HBM4 characterization challenges). As a result, we mildly 
trim our CoWoS estimates for 2026 (77K vs. 90K previously), while leaving 2027E 
volumes unchanged. AMD could see upside in 2027 if MI450 starts ramping smoothly, 
given that its reliance on N2 process node for MI450 offers more capacity upside than 
other accelerators (all competing for limited N3P capacity). Venice CPU is also likely to 
consume meaningful CoWoS and 2.5D packaging capacity in 2H26 and 2027, with the 
bulk of the volume likely moving to ASE in 2027; this could be a source of upside to AMD 
CoWoS consumption in 2027, given increased datacenter CPU demand. AMD appears 
to also have multiple CPUs and gaming GPUs utilizing 2.5D packaging in its 2027 
pipeline, with most of the wafer-based solutions using ASE and one panel-based design 
leveraging PTI.

traction, and current forecasts indicate a strong ramp in 2027.

•CoPoS engineering work in progress, likely to be adopted in 2028 and to eventually 
replace CoWoS: Our research indicates that TSMC and ASE are progressing on their 
engineering work on CoPoS, with pilot line likely ready by 1H26. We believe there are 
still engineering challenges that need to be overcome, with the aim of finalizing the 
process flow and equipment list by end-26. We believe CoPoS is slated to be adopted in 
2028 (likely some versions of NVDA Feynman at the earliest) if engineering and pilot 
production proceed without any delay, and should eventually replace CoWoS for large-
size AI accelerator packages. At this point, we still believe there are multiple engineering 
challenges that need to be overcome (warpage, equipment compatibility, material 
selection, etc.) before the process can enter mass production. CoWoS will still be used for 
smaller package sizes, with other processors (server CPU, networking, even PC and 
mobile processors) starting to adopt CoWoS-like packages in the 2028-29 timeframe.
•CoWoP progressing on a parallel track, timing of introduction uncertain: CoWoP, 
an NVDA-led effort to simplify the CoWoS packaging structure, is progressing on a 
parallel track. Our recent supply chain checks indicate encouraging progress on 
engineering milestones so far between NVDA, OSAT partners such as ASE and PCB 
vendors (Unimicron, ZDT, etc.). Key benefits of CoWoP include: (1) a more elegant 
package design that can eliminate the substrate layer completely; (2) hence increasing the 
signal integrity; (3) better thermal performance; and (4) much lower testing time, where 
increased chip probing and board-level testing coverage can eliminate significant 
amounts of final test and SLT. We are still not certain of the timing of introduction into 
mass production; we believe a small volume ramp of Rubin/Rubin Ultra 2-die solutions 
will be trialed using CoWoP at end-26/1H27 before any call on mass production ramp is 
made.

Table 5:  Glossary of important terms
HBM High Bandwidth MemoryHBM is a high-speed computer memory interface for 3D-stacked synchronous dynamic random-access memory (SDRAM) from Samsung, Micron
