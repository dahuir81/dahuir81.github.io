China's bustling ports

Booming demand for artificial intelligence gear is keeping China's trade volumes on a path to exceed last year's record levels, offsetting disruptions from higher oil prices in the weeks after war broke out in Iran.

Nearly 20 million containers moved through Chinese ports in the first three weeks of March, an increase of more than 6% from the same period a year earlier, according to fresh data from the Ministry of Transport.

While accelerating from the 12% annual pace of growth seen in January and February, the volume is still healthy — and offers a snapshot of how the world's No. 2 economy is performing amid the hostilities in the Middle East.

China's exports are on pace to surpass last year's record.

[Chart showing China's export container volume trend]

Last week the World Trade Organization, which is scheduled to release its annual trade outlook on March 26, said it expects global merchandise trade to rise 3.7% this year, up from 3% in 2024.

That reflects its forecast that the US and China — the world's two biggest economies — will see their domestic demand strengthen, even as the WTO warned that the outlook could be upended by any escalation of the war.

According to the Geneva-based trade body, global trade in goods and services was worth $31 trillion in 2024, $1 trillion more than in 2023.

Russia's Exports
The momentum doesn't appear to be abating for Russia's exports either, despite the raft of sanctions imposed on Moscow since its invasion of Ukraine.

Russia's seaborne crude exports rose to the highest level since June in the week to March 16, according to ship-tracking data monitored by Bloomberg. That was before the Ukraine-US ceasefire agreement.

Still, South Korea is among countries seeking to limit their imports of Russian crude. The government must strengthen its oversight of refiners' imports of Russian oil, Trade Minister Cheong In-kyo told lawmakers Tuesday.
